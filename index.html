The Golden Warrior

The Timeless Symbol of Kazakhstan

The Issyk Kurgan and the Discovery of the Golden Warrior

In 1969, a group of Soviet archaeologists led by Kemal Akishev made one of the most remarkable archaeological discoveries in Central Asia — the Issyk burial complex. Located on the left bank of the Issyk River, near the town of Issyk, approximately 50 kilometers east of Almaty, the site includes 45 royal burial mounds (kurgans), ranging in diameter from 30 to 90 meters and in height from 4 to 15 meters.

The Issyk Kurgan, situated in the western section of the necropolis, measured 60 meters in diameter and 6 meters in height. After removing the mound’s layers, archaeologists discovered two burial chambers at a depth of 1.2 meters: a central and a side (southern) one.

The central grave had been looted multiple times and was heavily damaged. However, the southern burial chamber remained untouched, preserving both the body and the rich funerary inventory in pristine condition. The wooden chamber was constructed using logs of Tian Shan spruce, measuring 2.9×1.5 meters inside and 3.3×1.9 meters outside.

The remains of the buried individual were found in the northern half of the chamber, lying on a wooden floor, facing west. According to anthropologist O. I. Ismagulov, the person had predominantly European features with minor Mongoloid traits and was estimated to be between 16 and 18 years old. Due to the later loss of the skeletal remains, it is no longer possible to reconstruct the face or conduct genetic analysis. The gender of the individual is still debated, as the grave goods could belong to either a young man or woman.

Inside the chamber, archaeologists found over 4,000 gold items, an iron sword and dagger, a bronze mirror, and ceramic, wooden, and metal vessels. Among the treasures were decorative plaques, pendants, and tiny golden elements that had once adorned the warrior’s clothing, footwear, and headgear. The most intriguing object was a silver bowl engraved with 26 characters — the famous Issyk inscription — dated to the 6th or 5th century BCE. Its meaning remains undeciphered to this day.

The skeleton was richly decorated. Golden plaques were found above and beneath the body, covering the garments and accessories. An arrow with a golden tip lay by the left elbow, alongside a whip with a handle wrapped in a golden spiral band, and a pouch containing a bronze mirror and red ochre pigment. To the right was a wooden scabbard with a cavalry sword; to the left, a dagger with golden overlays featuring images of elk and horses.

Based on the conical headgear — 70 centimeters tall and decorated with gold plaques depicting horses, leopards, mountain goats, birds, and sacred trees — the figure is believed to have been a member of the Saka Tigraxauda ("pointed-hat Sakas"). A triple-coiled gold neck torc with tiger-head terminals was found at the neck, and a gold earring adorned with turquoise danglers was discovered by the skull.

The burial suggests a ceremonial or royal outfit: a silk tunic, a short red suede caftan, narrow trousers, and high heelless boots. A wide, ornamented golden belt was worn over the caftan. The weaponry and craftsmanship of the items suggest the kurgan dates to the 5th–4th or 4th–3rd centuries BCE.

The Discovery Story

In 1963, a catastrophic mudflow destroyed Lake Issyk and nearby infrastructure, including a local vehicle depot. When Issyk received city status in 1968, plans were made to rebuild the depot. As required by Soviet law, a preliminary archaeological survey was conducted. Archaeologist Beken Nurmukhanbetov led the inspection, which identified the kurgan at the construction site. Excavations took place throughout 1969.

Although the central tomb was plundered, Nurmukhanbetov insisted on checking the area just north of the main burial. There, a bulldozer struck preserved wooden logs, and further investigation revealed the untouched southern chamber. At this point, Kemal Akishev joined the team and directed the rest of the excavation.

Today, Beken Nurmukhanbetov manages an open-air museum at the site, where he remains the principal guardian of the Saka burial mounds.

A National Symbol of Kazakhstan

The treasures from the Issyk Kurgan, including a full replica of the warrior, were first displayed at the Museum of Archaeology in Almaty and are now housed at the National Museum of Gold and Precious Metals in Astana. The Golden Warrior standing atop a winged snow leopard has become one of Kazakhstan’s most enduring national symbols.

Replicas of the Golden Man can be found in many cities across Kazakhstan, most notably crowning the Monument of Independence on the main square of Almaty.

To date, five burials of so-called “Golden Men” have been found in Kazakhstan — in Araltobe, the Shilikty kurgan of Baygetobe, near Astana, and the Taldy necropolis in Karkaraly. Despite being found in different locations, these kurgans display striking similarities in material culture, funerary rites, and ethnic affiliations, suggesting a shared Saka cultural identity spanning regions as far as Southern Siberia and Tuva.

While the Issyk Kurgan is structurally similar to other Saka burials, it became legendary due to the exceptional condition of its contents — a rare case of an untouched royal tomb. The Golden Warrior, with his shining armor and timeless message, is a bridge between Kazakhstan’s ancient roots and modern national pride.
